CONDITIONALS

First, Second & Third Conditional — If Clauses

What is a Conditional?

A conditional sentence describes a condition (if something happens) and a result (then something else happens).

Two parts:

IF-clause = the condition (the "if" part)

MAIN clause = the result (the "then" part)

Example: If it rains, I will stay home.

There are 4 types. We will learn the 3 most important ones!

The 4 Types — Overview

TypeIF-clauseMAIN clauseUse
0thpresentpresentAlways true (facts)
1stpresentwill + baseReal/possible future
2ndpast simplewould + baseUnreal present/future
3rdpast perfectwould have + ppUnreal past

Let's look at each one in detail →

1st Conditional — Form

Structure:

If + present simple, will + base verb

OR

Will + base verb + if + present simple

Comma Rule

✅ If it rains, I will stay home. comma

✅ I will stay home if it rains. no comma

Other words instead of "will"

You can also use: can, may, might, should, must

Example: "If you practice, you might improve quickly."

1st Conditional — When Do We Use It?

For real or possible situations in the future — things that might actually happen.

Uses:

• Promises: "If you come to my concert, I will give you a free T-shirt."

• Warnings: "If you don't wear sunscreen, you will get burned."

• Offers: "If you need help, I can carry your guitar."

Key idea

The condition is possible. It's not a dream — it could really happen.

"If the concert starts at 8, we will arrive early." → The concert really starts at 8. This is real.

1st Conditional — Examples

🎵 If I save enough money, I will buy tickets to the Salzburg Festival.

🏔️ If we hike the Eagle's Walk, we will see amazing views.

🎸 If she practices daily, she will become a great musician.

🌧️ If it stops raining, we will go hiking tomorrow.

Notice: IF-clause = present tense, MAIN clause = will + base verb

1st Conditional — Common Mistakes

❌ "If it will rain, I will stay home."

✅ "If it rains, I will stay home."

💡 After "if" we use present simple, NOT "will" — even though we're talking about the future. The "if" clause is always present tense in the 1st conditional.

❌ "I will buy a guitar if I will have enough money."

✅ "I will buy a guitar if I have enough money."

💡 Same rule — the "if" clause uses present tense. "Will have" in the if-clause is always wrong.

❌ "If you will practice, you improve."

✅ "If you practice, you will improve."

💡 The main clause needs "will" to show the future result. Without "will", it sounds like a general fact (0th conditional), not a future plan.

1st Conditional — Exercise

Q1: "If she _____ to the concert, she _____ a great time."

  • A) will go / will have
  • B) goes / will have
  • C) goes / has
  • D) will go / has
✅ B) goes / will have — Correct! Present simple in IF-clause, will + base in main clause.
❌ A) "will go" after "if" is wrong — never use "will" in the IF-clause.
❌ C) "has" is present, but we need "will have" to show the future result.
❌ D) Two mistakes: "will go" after "if" is wrong, AND "has" doesn't show future result.

Q2: "We _____ hiking if it _____ raining."

  • A) go / will stop
  • B) will go / stops
  • C) will go / will stop
  • D) go / stops
✅ B) will go / stops — Correct! Main clause first (no comma), present simple after "if".
❌ A) "will stop" after "if" is wrong — never use "will" in the IF-clause.
❌ C) Two "will" clauses — the second one (after "if") is wrong.
❌ D) Missing "will" in the main clause — this sounds like a habit, not a future plan.

Q3: "If he _____ guitar lessons, he _____ better."

  • A) takes / will get
  • B) will take / gets
  • C) takes / gets
  • D) took / will get
✅ A) takes / will get — Correct! Present simple + will + base verb.
❌ B) "will take" after "if" is wrong.
❌ C) "gets" makes it a general fact (0th conditional), not a future possibility.
❌ D) "took" is past tense — that would be 2nd conditional, not 1st.

2nd Conditional — Form

Structure:

If + past simple, would + base verb

⚠️ Important!

The past tense here does NOT mean past time! It means "unreal / distant from reality".

If I had a million euros, I would buy a recording studio.

→ I don't have a million euros. This is imaginary / hypothetical.

"Were" rule

Use "were" for all subjects (I/he/she/it) in formal English:

✅ "If I were a rock star..." (not "was")

⚠️ "If I was..." is common in informal speech, but "were" is grammatically correct.

2nd Conditional — When Do We Use It?

For unreal or unlikely situations in the present or future — things that probably won't happen, but we like to imagine.

Uses:

• Dreams / wishes: "If I had more free time, I would learn the violin." (But I don't have free time)

• Advice (imaginary): "If I were you, I would practice more." (But I'm not you)

• Unlikely scenarios: "If I won the lottery, I would travel the world." (Very unlikely)

Key idea

The condition is not real right now. We're imagining a different present.

2nd Conditional — Examples

🚁 If I had a helicopter, I would fly over the Austrian Alps.

🎤 If she were a famous singer, she would perform at the Vienna State Opera.

🏔️ If we lived closer to the mountains, we would hike every weekend.

🥁 If he played the drums, he would join a band.

Notice: IF-clause = past tense, MAIN clause = would + base verb

But the meaning is present/future, not past!

2nd Conditional — Common Mistakes

❌ "If I would have more time, I would travel."

✅ "If I had more time, I would travel."

💡 Never use "would" in the IF-clause. Only use "would" in the main clause. The IF-clause uses past simple (had).

❌ "If I was you, I would study more."

✅ "If I were you, I would study more."

💡 In the 2nd conditional, use "were" for all subjects (formal). "Was" is common in informal speech but "were" is grammatically correct.

❌ "If she has more money, she would buy a guitar."

✅ "If she had more money, she would buy a guitar."

💡 2nd conditional needs past simple in the IF-clause, not present. "Has" is present tense — that creates a mixed conditional (which is wrong here).

2nd Conditional — Exercise

Q1: "If I _____ a famous musician, I _____ all over the world."

  • A) am / will tour
  • B) were / would tour
  • C) was / will tour
  • D) were / will tour
✅ B) were / would tour — Correct! Past tense (were) + would + base verb for unreal present.
❌ A) Present tense (am) + will = 1st conditional, but "a famous musician" suggests this is hypothetical/unreal.
❌ C) "was" is informal (should be "were"), AND "will" doesn't match the hypothetical meaning — need "would".
❌ D) "were" is correct, but "will" doesn't match the unreal meaning — need "would".

Q2: "If he _____ the mountains more often, he _____ happier."

  • A) climbs / will be
  • B) climbed / would be
  • C) climbed / would have been
  • D) will climb / will be
✅ B) climbed / would be — Correct! Past simple + would + base for unreal present.
❌ A) Present tense = 1st conditional (real possibility), but context suggests he doesn't climb much (unreal).
❌ C) "would have been" is 3rd conditional (past), but this is about present/general feeling.
❌ D) "will" in the IF-clause is always wrong.

Q3: "If we _____ more, we _____ better musicians."

  • A) practice / become
  • B) practiced / would become
  • C) practiced / became
  • D) will practice / become
✅ B) practiced / would become — Correct! Past simple + would + base for unreal present.
❌ A) Present tense = 0th conditional (general fact), not hypothetical.
❌ C) "became" is past, not "would + verb" — this breaks the 2nd conditional structure.
❌ D) "will" in the IF-clause is always wrong.

3rd Conditional — Form

Structure:

If + past perfect (had + past participle), would have + past participle

Meaning

Talking about an unreal past — imagining a different outcome for something that already happened.

If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.

→ I didn't study hard. I failed. I'm imagining a different past.

Key rule

You CANNOT change the past. The 3rd conditional is only for imagining alternatives — regrets, criticism, and "what if..."

3rd Conditional — When Do We Use It?

For unreal situations in the past — things that cannot be changed.

Uses:

• Regrets: "If I had woken up earlier, I would have caught the train." (But I didn't — I missed it)

• Criticism: "You should have practiced more." (You didn't)

• "What if...": "If I had known about the concert, I would have gone." (But I didn't know)

Key idea

The 3rd conditional is about the past that cannot be changed. We use it to imagine how things could have been different.

3rd Conditional — Examples

🎸 If I had started guitar lessons at age 10, I would have become a better player by now.

🚂 If we had taken the earlier train, we would have reached the summit before sunset.

🎶 If she had listened to her teacher, she would have improved faster.

🌦️ If it hadn't rained, we would have finished the hike.

Notice: IF-clause = had + past participle, MAIN clause = would have + past participle

3rd Conditional — Common Mistakes

❌ "If I would have went to the concert, I would have seen them."

✅ "If I had gone to the concert, I would have seen them."

💡 Never use "would have" in the IF-clause. Use "had + past participle" (had gone). Also "went" is past simple — you need the past participle "gone".

❌ "If she has practiced more, she would have won."

✅ "If she had practiced more, she would have won."

💡 "Has practiced" is present perfect. The 3rd conditional needs past perfect (had practiced) because we're talking about an unreal past.

❌ "If he studied harder, he would have passed."

✅ "If he had studied harder, he would have passed."

💡 "Studied" is past simple (2nd conditional form). For past unreal situations, you need past perfect (had studied).

3rd Conditional — Exercise + Summary

Q1: "If I _____ about the concert, I _____ tickets."

  • A) knew / would buy
  • B) had known / would have bought
  • C) had known / would buy
  • D) have known / would have bought
✅ B) had known / would have bought — Correct! Past perfect + would have + pp for unreal past.
❌ A) "knew / would buy" is 2nd conditional (present unreal), but the context suggests past regret.
❌ C) "had known" is correct, but "would buy" is 2nd conditional result — doesn't match the past context.
❌ D) "have known" is present perfect, not past perfect.

Q2: "If they _____ the trail map, they _____ lost."

  • A) brought / wouldn't get
  • B) had brought / wouldn't have gotten
  • C) had brought / wouldn't get
  • D) have brought / wouldn't have gotten
✅ B) had brought / wouldn't have gotten — Correct! Past perfect + would have + pp.
❌ A) 2nd conditional form, but context is past (they got lost).
❌ C) "wouldn't get" is present/future, doesn't match past context.
❌ D) "have brought" is present perfect, not past perfect.

Q3: "If she _____ the guitar competition, she _____ first place."

  • A) entered / would win
  • B) had entered / would have won
  • C) had entered / will have won
  • D) enters / will win
✅ B) had entered / would have won — Correct! Past perfect + would have + pp.
❌ A) 2nd conditional, but context is a past competition.
❌ C) "will have won" doesn't exist in standard conditional structures.
❌ D) 1st conditional (future), but context is past competition.
