GERUND vs INFINITIVE

"-ing" or "to + verb"? — The Complete Guide

Press → or Space to start

What Are They?

Gerund: verb + -ing (swimming, playing, hiking) — acts like a noun

Infinitive: to + verb (to swim, to play, to hike) — can act like noun, adjective, or adverb

Both can be the subject or object of a sentence:

Swimming is fun. = To swim is fun. ✅ (both OK, gerund more common)

Playing guitar takes practice. = To play guitar takes practice. ✅

The big question: which one to use after each verb? Let's find out →

Let's look at each category in detail →

Verbs Followed by GERUND Only

These verbs are always followed by -ing. Never infinitive!

Common verbs:

enjoy, finish, stop, quit, mind, miss, suggest, admit, deny, avoid, consider, imagine, keep, practice, risk, give up, put off, look forward to, be worth, can't stand, can't help, it's no use, there's no point in

Examples

🎸 "I enjoy playing guitar." ✅ (NOT "enjoy to play")

🎶 "She finished practicing at 6pm."

🏔️ "We look forward to hiking in the Alps."

🚫 "He gave up smoking last year."

⚠️ "Look forward to" — the "to" here is a preposition, so it takes gerund!

Verbs Followed by INFINITIVE Only

These verbs are always followed by to + base verb. Never gerund!

Common verbs:

want, need, decide, hope, plan, promise, afford, agree, arrange, attempt, claim, demand, expect, fail, hesitate, learn, manage, offer, prepare, pretend, refuse, seem, threaten, wait, wish, would like, would love, would hate, would prefer

Examples

🎹 "I want to learn the piano." ✅ (NOT "want learning")

🎤 "She decided to join a band."

🏔️ "We plan to hike the Dolomites this summer."

⏰ "He promised to practice every day."

Both — No Meaning Change

With these verbs, gerund and infinitive mean the same thing. You can use either!

Verbs: start, begin, continue, like, love, hate, prefer, can't bear, can't stand, intend, bother, cease, dread, commence

Examples

🌧️ "It started raining" = "It started to rain" ✅

🏔️ "I like hiking" = "I like to hike" ✅

🎸 "She loves playing" = "She loves to play" ✅

Small difference with "prefer"

"I prefer hiking" (general preference) vs "I prefer to hike now" (specific situation)

Both — MEANING CHANGE! ⚠️

Same verb, different meaning depending on gerund or infinitive:

STOP

Gerund: "He stopped smoking" = he quit the habit

Infinitive: "He stopped to smoke" = he paused in order to smoke (purpose)

REMEMBER

Gerund: "I remember locking the door" = I have a memory of doing it (past)

Infinitive: "I remember to lock the door" = I didn't forget (future obligation)

FORGET

Gerund: "I forget meeting him" = I don't have a memory of it

Infinitive: "I forgot to meet him" = I didn't remember to do it (didn't do it)

TRY

Gerund: "I tried playing the guitar" = I experimented with it

Infinitive: "I tried to play the guitar" = I made an effort (maybe it was hard)

REGRET

Gerund: "I regret telling her" = I'm sorry about a past action

Infinitive: "I regret to tell you" = I'm sorry, but I must inform you (formal)

Gerund as Subject

Gerunds often work as the subject of a sentence.

Gerund as subject = more common and natural (everyday English)

Infinitive as subject = more formal/literary (essays, books)

Examples

🏔️ "Hiking in the Alps is my favorite hobby." (natural)

🏔️ "To hike in the Alps is my favorite hobby." (formal)

🎸 "Playing an instrument takes years of practice." (natural)

🎸 "To play an instrument takes years of practice." (formal)

💡 Tip: In everyday English, use gerund as subject. In essays/formal writing, infinitive is OK.

After Prepositions — ALWAYS Gerund!

After a preposition, ALWAYS use gerund (-ing). Never infinitive!

Common prepositions + gerund:

interested in learning, good at playing, excited about hiking, insisted on practicing, tired of waiting, afraid of flying, proud of winning, responsible for organizing, used to waking up early

Examples

🎵 "I'm interested in learning music production." ✅

🎸 "She's good at playing the guitar."

🏔️ "We're excited about hiking this weekend."

⏰ "He insisted on practicing every day."

Exception

"but" and "except" can take infinitive: "I had no choice but to wait." / "She does nothing except complain."

Common Mistakes

❌ "I enjoy to play guitar."

✅ "I enjoy playing guitar."

💡 "Enjoy" is always followed by gerund. Never infinitive. No exceptions!

❌ "She wants going to the concert."

✅ "She wants to go to the concert."

💡 "Want" is always followed by infinitive. Never gerund.

❌ "I'm looking forward to see you."

✅ "I'm looking forward to seeing you."

💡 The "to" in "look forward to" is a preposition, not the infinitive marker. After prepositions, use gerund (-ing)!

❌ "He suggested to go hiking."

✅ "He suggested going hiking."

💡 "Suggest" is always followed by gerund. Never infinitive.

❌ "I stopped to rest for an hour." (if you mean the resting stopped)

✅ "I stopped resting for an hour."

💡 "Stop + to + verb" = "stop in order to..." (purpose). "Stop + gerund" = the action itself stopped. Be careful with meaning!

Exercise: Gerund or Infinitive?

Q1: "I enjoy _____ (listen) to music while I study."

  • A) listen
  • B) to listen
  • C) listening
  • D) listened
✅ C) listening — Correct! "Enjoy" is always followed by gerund.
❌ A) "listen" is base form — needs "to" or "-ing" after "enjoy".
❌ B) "to listen" — "enjoy" takes gerund only, never infinitive.
❌ D) "listened" is past tense — wrong form after "enjoy".

Q2: "She decided _____ (buy) a new guitar."

  • A) buying
  • B) to buy
  • C) buy
  • D) bought
✅ B) to buy — Correct! "Decide" is always followed by infinitive.
❌ A) "buying" — "decide" takes infinitive only, never gerund.
❌ C) "buy" is missing "to" — infinitive needs "to".
❌ D) "bought" is past tense — wrong form after "decide".

Q3: "We stopped _____ (rest) for an hour." (We paused in order to rest)

  • A) resting
  • B) to rest
  • C) rest
  • D) rested
✅ B) to rest — Correct! "Stop + to + verb" = purpose (we stopped in order to rest).
❌ A) "resting" means the resting itself stopped (he quit resting).
❌ C) "rest" is missing "to" or "-ing".
❌ D) "rested" is past tense — wrong form.

Q4: "He suggested _____ (go) to the concert."

  • A) to go
  • B) going
  • C) go
  • D) went
✅ B) going — Correct! "Suggest" is always followed by gerund.
❌ A) "to go" — "suggest" takes gerund only, never infinitive.
❌ C) "go" is missing "to" or "-ing".
❌ D) "went" is past tense — wrong form.

Q5: "I'm looking forward to _____ (see) you at the festival."

  • A) see
  • B) to see
  • C) seeing
  • D) saw
✅ C) seeing — Correct! "To" in "look forward to" is a preposition, so it takes gerund.
❌ A) "see" — after preposition "to", need gerund.
❌ B) "to see" — the "to" in "look forward to" is NOT the infinitive marker. It's a preposition!
❌ D) "saw" is past tense — wrong form.

Q6: "They want _____ (climb) the mountain before sunrise."

  • A) climbing
  • B) to climb
  • C) climb
  • D) climbed
✅ B) to climb — Correct! "Want" is always followed by infinitive.
❌ A) "climbing" — "want" takes infinitive only, never gerund.
❌ C) "climb" is missing "to" — infinitive needs "to".
❌ D) "climbed" is past tense — wrong form.

Exercise — Meaning Change

Q1: "I remember _____ (lock) the door before I left." (I have a memory of doing it)

  • A) locking
  • B) to lock
✅ A) locking — Correct! "Remember + gerund" = memory of a past action.
❌ B) "to lock" means "I didn't forget to lock it" (future obligation), not a memory.

Q2: "Please remember _____ (lock) the door when you leave." (Don't forget!)

  • A) locking
  • B) to lock
✅ B) to lock — Correct! "Remember + infinitive" = don't forget to do it (future obligation).
❌ A) "locking" would mean you have a memory of locking it (past action), not a reminder.

Q3: "He stopped _____ (smoke) because of his health." (He quit)

  • A) smoking
  • B) to smoke
✅ A) smoking — Correct! "Stop + gerund" = the habit/action stopped.
❌ B) "to smoke" means he paused in order to smoke (purpose), which contradicts "because of his health".

Q4: "She stopped _____ (drink) water because she was thirsty." (She paused to get water)

  • A) drinking
  • B) to drink
✅ B) to drink — Correct! "Stop + infinitive" = purpose (she stopped in order to drink).
❌ A) "drinking" means she quit drinking water, which doesn't make sense with "because she was thirsty".

Special Cases & Tips

GO ON

Gerund: "She went on playing for hours." = she continued playing

Infinitive: "She went on to play another song." = she then played (next action)

BE USED TO vs USED TO

"I'm used to waking up early." = it's normal for me now (preposition "to" → gerund)

"I used to wake up early." = I did in the past but not anymore (infinitive)

HAVE TROUBLE / DIFFICULTY

"I have trouble sleeping." / "She had difficulty finding the note." (implied preposition "in" → gerund)

WOULD RATHER / HAD BETTER

"I'd rather go hiking than stay home." (bare infinitive — no "to")

"You'd better practice more." (bare infinitive — no "to")

Final Summary

Quick Decision Tree

1. After a preposition? → GERUND

2. After enjoy/finish/stop/mind/suggest/avoid/consider/keep/practice/give up/look forward to? → GERUND

3. After want/need/decide/hope/plan/promise/afford/learn/manage/offer/refuse/seem? → INFINITIVE

4. After start/begin/continue/like/love/hate/prefer? → BOTH (same meaning)

5. After stop/remember/forget/try/regret/go on? → BOTH (meaning changes!)

Golden Rules

🎯 Gerund = focuses on the experience / action itself

🎯 Infinitive = focuses on the purpose / intention / future

🎯 When in doubt, read it aloud — your ear often knows!
